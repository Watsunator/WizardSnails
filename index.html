The White Wizard Snail

All about Filapaludina Martensi, the freshwater, livebearing, trapdoor, fully aquatic snail known simply as the Wizard Snail!

What on earth is a wizard snail?

So glad you asked! White Wizard Snails are a white version of... wizard snails! Ah, but, you're here asking what... what any wizard snails are. Not everyone cares about the science, so I'll give two options- the science information and the basic information.

White Wizards are-

They are a freshwater trapdoor snail, and have multiple sub-species color morphs. They are in the Viviparidae family. They have an operculum, gills that can filter feed, and they give live birth to tiny, perfectly formed baby snails. They can burrow as a stress, after birthing, or even sometimes seemingly to rest a bit. While they "can" filter feed, it is often misconstrued as their primary method of eating. These guys will, excruciatingly slowly, eat their way all around their environment. They come from Thailand, Vietnam, Cambodia, the Philippines, Malaysia, Laos, and Indonesia. Primarily they are found in canals and ponds. Online searches say that the types need updated names, but scientifically the current three types are Filopaludina martensi cambodiensis, Filopaludina martensi martensi, and Filopaludina martensi munensis. Now, I could not find concrete proof as to which scientific name belongs to which coloration. However, the most common snail sold under the name Filapaludina Martensi, is the White Wizard snail. They have a naturally light/white shell, often with some natural cream or tan coloration and generally have a beautiful blue/black tip to the sworl of their shell. One type is FREQUENTLY misconstrued as the piano snail, but is not, the primary visible difference is the coloration and thickness of the stripes. It is called commonly the Striped Wizard Snail. And lastly, is a snail that is various shades of brown that may or may not have fine striping on its shell but not to the same level as the striped wizard snail.

This freshwater type of snail has a "trapdoor" that is attached to their body that acts as a door when they hide inside their shell. They require two snails to make babies, and the female snails form the babies inside her shell, giving live birth usually at night. These snails can burrow into sand when they're stressed, tired, and sometimes before/after birthing. These snails are beautiful, but not very fast. When you get one, you should drip acclimate them before adding them to your tank so they can adjust to your water parameters/chemistry. These snails have a foot that can be a bluey/black tint on parts of the flesh, but mostly a light tan to creamy white coloration. Their nose and antenna are a velvety looking blue/black coloration, and their whole body and antenna have beautiful orange and gold flecking. The art on this webpage is modeled after the most common type to be sold, the White Wizard! Many of us hobbysists who get these name at least one Gandalf. They have a white, or creamy colored shell usually with a dark velvety colored sworl tip. Like many slower snails, they may end up getting an almost green patina from algae growth, and some may even get a soft algae afro like many other aquatic snail species. This does not hurt them! Once they adjust to your water chemistry, they move a lot and are very active, but they are sensitive to bad water quality or super soft water. There are 3 types of wizard snails, all three have the beautiful velvet faces and foot coloration. One is white, one is brown and striped with thin beautiful stripes, and the third are brown and can come in various shades of brown.

What do these snails need to survive in our home aquariums?

So, this can depend on a few things here. Firstly, these guys need a higher ph. Personally I recommend 7.5+ but I've seen people who own them and keep them in less. You want calcium in both the water, and their diet. Soft water can erode all snail shells over time, it's a frequent problem you see in a lot of hobbyist social and help spaces. pH chasing however, is almost always a bad idea for our inhabitants. Stable water parameters are the best. Cuttlefish bone, and various forms of snail, shrimp, and crab foods and supplements can aid in their diets.

These guys have heavier shells, and are much slower moving than bladder, pond, ramshorn, or mystery snails. I have seen them thrive in higher water flow, and very low water flow. But you should ensure you have enough plants and surface agitation to provide sufficient oxygem in your aquarium. Unlike some of our aquatic snails, these guys don't surface much outside of eating food at the surface.

As a final note on the shells of these guys, unfortunately, it is common to buy a White Wizard, and your snail may genetically be a plain brown snail that has either been acid dipped to eat off the outer layers of shell, or sanded to sand off the outer layer of shell. It appears, but is currently unproven, that there is even a visible difference in the babies of genetically white wizards, and genetically brown wizard babies. They both have these beautiful purple stripes on the shells, but it appears white wizards that are truly white and have mated with another white wizard, the babies will be pale yellow almost transluscent with purple stripes, and the brown babies will have various darker coloration with purple stripes. Some of my brown wizard babies have been almost a purply mahagony coloration. Sadly, it also appears that snails carrying brown and white genetics may be sold to you in the same batch so it can be difficult or near impossible to tell, as in general wizard snails in healthy shell condition have beautiful smooth looking shells. Someone who bought from the same seller as me had their white wizard in the same local water grow out beautiful white shell that matched the rest of the snail's color, and my white wizards had deep black pitting at first with new growth, and then had brown shell grow in.
